How to A/B Compare Mixes Online (No Plugin Required)

You've bounced two versions of a mix. One has the vocal up half a dB, the other has a wider stereo spread on the chorus. You know one is better — but after switching back and forth in your DAW for ten minutes, you can't tell anymore. Your ears have given up. Here's why that happens, and how to fix it.

The 3-Second Problem

Your ear has a surprisingly short memory. Research on auditory perception shows that after about 3 seconds, your brain begins to "normalize" what it's hearing. The difference between version A and version B starts to dissolve. By the time you've stopped one file, scrolled to the same spot in another, and hit play, the comparison is already compromised.

This is why professional mastering engineers don't just listen to tracks back-to-back — they use instant switching. Same playback position, zero gap, seamless cut. That's the only way to hear what actually changed between two versions.

Why Your DAW Isn't Ideal for This

Most engineers default to lining up two bounces on adjacent tracks in their DAW and soloing between them. It works, but it has real problems.

First, level differences lie to you. A mix that's even 0.5 dB louder will sound "better" to your brain — punchier, clearer, more exciting. If your two bounces aren't loudness-matched, every comparison is biased from the start. And matching loudness by hand in a DAW is tedious.

Second, you can't share it. If you're working with a collaborator, a client, or a vocalist who lives in another city, they can't open your DAW session. You end up emailing two files with a message like "listen to 2:15 in both and tell me which low end you prefer." They'll say "I think the second one?" and you'll spend the rest of the day wondering if they actually compared the right section.

Plugin-Based Solutions

Tools like ABmyMix and ADPTR Streamliner solve the instant-switching problem inside the DAW. They're good for personal use — load two files, flip between them, done. ABmyMix in particular is a clean, focused tool that does this well.

But plugin-based tools share the same limitation: the comparison lives on your machine. If your collaborator doesn't have the plugin, or isn't using the same DAW, or isn't technical enough to set it up, you're back to emailing two separate files.

The Browser-Based Approach

What if the comparison itself was shareable? That's the idea behind browser-based A/B tools. You upload two files, set up the comparison, and get a link. Your collaborator opens it in any browser — on their phone, on their laptop, wherever — and hears exactly what you set up. Same playback position, same loop points, same loudness matching.

No plugin to install. No DAW required. No "which file is which?" confusion.

Why this matters

The goal isn't to replace your DAW workflow. It's to extend it. You still mix in your DAW. But when it's time to get feedback from someone who isn't sitting in your studio, a shareable comparison removes all the friction.

How to A/B Compare Online with Bounced

Bounced is a free browser-based tool built specifically for this workflow. Here's how it works:

Upload two bounces. Drag in version A and version B. WAV, MP3, or FLAC — any format your browser can play.

Playback auto-syncs. Both files are aligned from the start. Hit play and you're hearing version A. Hit the switch button (or press a keyboard shortcut) and you're hearing version B — at the exact same timecode. No gap.

Loudness matching. Bounced analyzes the integrated loudness of both files and matches them automatically. You're comparing the mix, not the level.

Loop a section. Drag a region on the waveform to loop a specific passage — the chorus, the bridge, the low end at 1:45. The comparison stays locked to that section.

Share the link. Generate a URL and send it. Your collaborator opens it and gets the exact same comparison — same files, same loop, same loudness match. They switch between A and B in their own browser and tell you what they hear.

When to Use A/B Comparison

A/B switching isn't something you do on every mix. It's most useful at specific decision points in the production process:

Mix revisions. "I pushed the vocals up half a dB and widened the chorus." Does the client hear the difference? Do they prefer it? A shareable A/B link answers this in 30 seconds instead of a 10-email thread.

Mastering approval. Comparing the pre-master and post-master back to back, loudness-matched, is the cleanest way to hear exactly what mastering added (or removed).

Reference checking. Load your mix against a commercial reference track to check balance, width, and overall vibe. The level matching is especially important here — commercial masters are usually louder than your mix bus output.

Gear and plugin shootouts. Did that $200 compressor plugin actually change anything? Load the bypassed and processed versions and switch. Your ears will tell you faster than any analyzer.

What Bounced Doesn't Do

Bounced isn't a DAW. It doesn't record, it doesn't process, it doesn't mix. It does one thing: lets you upload two files, compare them instantly, and share that comparison with someone else. If you need deep analysis, spectral display, or mid-side monitoring, use your DAW or a dedicated plugin. Bounced is for the moment when you need someone else to hear what you're hearing.
