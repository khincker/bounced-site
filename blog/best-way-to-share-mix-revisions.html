The Best Way to Share Mix Revisions with Clients and Collaborators

You finished the revision at 1 AM. Pushed the vocals up, tightened the low end, widened the chorus. You bounce it, attach it to an email, and type "let me know what you think." Three days later your client responds: "I think I liked the old one better? Not sure." You have no idea what they actually compared, or if they compared anything at all.

The Real Problem with Mix Feedback

Sharing mix revisions isn't hard. You can email a WAV, drop it in Google Drive, upload it to Dropbox, send it via WeTransfer. The file gets there. That was never the problem.

The problem is what happens after the file arrives. Your client now has two audio files sitting in a folder. To compare them, they need to open both, find the same section in each, and switch between them fast enough to hear the difference. Most people don't do this. They listen to the new version once, form a vague impression, and respond based on that impression.

That's not a comparison. That's a guess.

Why Email Chains Don't Work

The typical mix revision workflow looks like this: you send a bounce, your collaborator listens on their phone while walking the dog, they reply with "sounds great but can you make the snare pop more?" You make the change, send another bounce. They listen again, this time on laptop speakers. They say "actually I think the first one was better."

The problem isn't the feedback — it's the listening conditions. Every time your collaborator opens a file in a different app, on different speakers, in a different environment, they're hearing something different. They're not comparing your revision to the previous version. They're comparing a memory of what the old version sounded like to what the new version sounds like right now.

And as we covered in our A/B comparison guide, auditory memory fades fast. After about 3 seconds, the brain starts normalizing what it hears. By the time someone opens a second file and scrubs to the same section, the comparison is already compromised.

The core issue

Most mix feedback isn't about the mix — it's about the gap between what the engineer heard and what the client heard. If you can close that gap, the feedback gets better immediately.

What Clients Actually Need

Your client doesn't need another file in their inbox. They need three things: the old version, the new version, and an easy way to switch between them at the exact same moment in the song. That's it. If they can flip between A and B at the chorus and hear the vocal difference instantly, they'll give you a real answer in 30 seconds.

The reason tools like ABmyMix exist is because engineers already know this. Instant switching, loudness matching, loop regions — these are standard comparison features. ABmyMix does this well in the browser for free. But sharing a comparison requires both users to connect Dropbox and already have access to the same files. Your client doesn't want to set up Dropbox integration to hear a mix revision. They need a comparison tool that meets them where they are — one click, one link, done.

File-Sharing Platforms vs. Comparison Tools

There are plenty of platforms built for sharing audio with collaborators. Filepass, Disco, Dropbox, SoundCloud private links — they all get the file from point A to point B. Some of them even let you leave timestamped comments, which is useful for detailed feedback.

But none of them solve the comparison problem. If your client is looking at two tracks on Filepass, they're still listening to one, stopping it, scrolling to the other, and playing it. The same broken workflow, just with a nicer interface around it.

A few platforms have added "version comparison" features — usually a toggle button that switches between uploads. That's a step in the right direction, but without synced playback, loudness matching, or loop regions, it's still not a real A/B test. It's two separate players with a button between them.

A Better Workflow: Send the Comparison, Not the File

The shift is simple: instead of sending your client a file to listen to, send them a comparison to listen through. They don't need to manage files, find the right section, or figure out which version is which. They just open a link and start switching.

When This Matters Most

Small, surgical revisions. You pushed the vocal up half a dB. You widened the stereo image on the chorus. You pulled 2 dB of low end out of the kick. These are the changes that disappear if you don't compare them instantly. A shareable A/B comp is the only way your client will actually hear what you did.

Mastering delivery. The difference between a pre-master and a finished master can be subtle. Sending both files separately almost guarantees your client won't hear it. An A/B comp with loudness matching lets the mastering speak for itself.

Multiple stakeholders. When a producer, an artist, and a label rep all need to weigh in on a mix, you need everyone hearing the same comparison. Not three people listening to different files on different devices forming three different opinions. One link, one comparison, one conversation.

Remote sessions. If your collaborator isn't sitting next to you, every revision round has a built-in communication tax. The clearer the comparison, the less back-and-forth. A/B links cut revision cycles because the feedback is based on what people actually heard, not what they remember hearing.

The bottom line

Bad feedback isn't your client's fault. It's a tool problem. If you give someone two files and ask them to compare, you're asking them to do something their ears aren't built for. Give them an instant comparison instead, and the feedback fixes itself.

What About Timestamped Comments?

Timestamped comment tools like Filepass and SoundCloud are great for detailed mix notes — "the reverb tail at 2:35 is too long," "bring the guitar up here." They solve a real problem, and if that's what you need, use them.

But timestamped comments and A/B comparison solve different problems. Comments are for detailed notes on a single version. A/B comparison is for the binary decision: which version is better? They work best together — compare, decide, then leave detailed notes on the winner.
